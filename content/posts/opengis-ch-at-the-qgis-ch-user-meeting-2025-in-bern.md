<p>This past <strong>Tuesday in Bern</strong>, the Swiss QGIS community came together for the 2025 edition of the <strong>QGIS.ch User Meeting</strong> — and we at <strong>OPENGIS.ch</strong> were proud to be deeply involved across the entire event, from presentations to hands-on workshops.</p>
<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Sharing Insights and Innovation</h2>
<p><span style="margin: 0px; padding: 0px;">The day began with our CEO, <strong>Marco Bernasocchi,</strong> opening the conference with an update on the <strong>QGIS project</strong>, covering exciting news about the upcoming <strong>QGIS 4</strong> release and the ongoing website revamp (slides <a href="https://slides.opengis.ch/talk-qgis.org/qgisch2025" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">here</a>).</span> Shortly after, he took the stage again to present the latest improvements in <strong>QField</strong>, including new features, user experience (UX) enhancements, and under-the-hood upgrades that continue to enable efficient field data collection (slides <a href="https://docs.google.com/presentation/d/1IMD93xeQy9aRbKWXdJDB8YvyKigZFLA8Llig37xLMro" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">here</a>).</p>
<p>In collaboration with <strong>Timothée Produit</strong> from IG Group SA, our colleague <strong>Isabel Kiefer</strong> presented tools and streamlined processes for i<strong>nstalling, managing, and updating TEKSI </strong>(and other) <strong>modules</strong>. These solutions are a testament to our mission of simplifying complex GIS infrastructure in public and private organisations alike.</p>
<p>Later in the morning, our CTO <strong>Mathias Kuhn</strong> gave a compelling talk on <strong>Machine Learning and AI in QGIS</strong>, showing real-world use cases and technical innovations that bridge geospatial workflows with intelligent automation.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Strengthening QGIS Security</h2>
<p>As part of our commitment to sustainability and professionalisation in open source GIS, we are also proud to be a <strong>partner of Oslandia in the <a href="https://security.qgis.oslandia.com/" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">QGIS Security Project</a></strong>, which Vincent Picavet presented during the event. This initiative aims to ensure that QGIS continues to meet the highest standards of security — a crucial foundation for its growing adoption in critical infrastructures around the world.</p>
<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Hands-on with QField – in Three Languages!</h2>
<p>In the afternoon, OPENGIS.ch hosted a fully booked, multilingual <strong>QField workshop</strong>, attended by 25 enthusiastic participants. <h2 class="wp-block-heading">OPENGIS.ch Tools in Action</h2>
<p>Even outside of our sessions, tools developed by OPENGIS.ch were featured prominently throughout the day:</p>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>QField</strong> played a key role in the <strong>Zermatt use case presentation</strong>, demonstrating its flexibility and robustness in alpine field operations.</li>
<li>The <strong>Model Baker plugin</strong>, to which we contribute heavily, was showcased with its new <strong>multilanguage support</strong> for QGIS models — a significant step forward for the Swiss context and its multilingual projects.</li>
</ul>
