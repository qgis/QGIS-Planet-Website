<p class="wp-block-paragraph">It’s been a couple of busy weeks, with the QGIS 4.2 release and meetings and conferences all over the place before a few, hopefully quieter, weeks of summer break.</p>
<h2 class="wp-block-heading">QGIS</h2>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">First, the Austrian QGIS user group met online on 25 June. The topic was webmapping, with multiple users presenting their webmapping solutions, ranging from Lizmap to QGIS Cloud.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">A few days later, <a href="https://www.qgis.org/project/visual-changelogs/visualchangelog42/">QGIS 4.2</a> was released on 3 July 2026. This release is named Belém do Pará, after the Brazilian city that hosted both FOSS4G and a QGIS user meeting back in 2024. <a href="https://mundogeo.com/en/2026/07/06/belem-do-para-lends-its-name-to-the-new-version-of-the-free-software-qgis/">MundoGEO has the details on the naming</a>, if you’re curious about the backstory. Worth noting: 4.2 “Belém do Pará” will be the next LTR, so if you’re using the long-term release, this is the one to watch for.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">On a side note, while designing the Belém splash screen, it was interesting to see that historic maps of Belém don’t have north at the top. Instead, they’re rotated with north pointing either left or right. A nice little reminder that “north-up” is a convention, not a law of cartography.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">AGIT 2026</h2>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">This week, <a href="https://fosstodon.org/@underdarkGIS/116879186534005902">I made my way to Salzburg</a> for <a href="https://agit.at">AGIT</a>, my favorite Austrian GIS conference.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">On the first day, I took part in the AGEO Podium discussion, together with Andreas Hocevar (the father of OpenLayers), on the OGC API standards, since many users aren’t even aware of these new standards yet.</p>

<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Thursday was talk day for me: I presented <a href="https://movingpandas.github.io/mobiml/">MobiML</a>, a new Python library designed to streamline the development of machine learning workflows for trajectory data. I hope this library can help make Mobility Data Science more approachable and results more reproducible.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Right after my talk, <a href="https://datasci.social/@mszll">Michael Szell</a> presented <a href="https://fosstodon.org/@underdarkGIS/116889850269375931">“Assessing the Danish Bicycle Node Network”</a>, building the data and algorithm foundation for active mobility planning and research. If you want to dig into the tools behind it, check out <a href="https://bikenetwork.dk">bikenetwork.dk</a> and <a href="https://bikenetkit.org">bikenetkit.org</a>.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Michael is <a href="https://csh.ac.at/events/the-complex-socio-technical-system-of-bicycle-networks-from-politics-to-planning-software/">giving a full talk on this</a> on Tuesday at the Complexity Science Hub in Vienna, if you want to hear more.</p>
<h2 class="wp-block-heading">FOSS4G Europe, from the sidelines</h2>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Unfortunately, I missed <a href="https://2026.europe.foss4g.org/">FOSS4G Europe</a> in Timișoara the week before, so I followed along via the <a href="https://fosstodon.org/tags/foss4ge2026">#foss4ge2026</a> hashtag instead. <a href="https://mastodon.social/@phanecak/116844745056680655">Iván Sánchez’s talk on the BOSCO ruling</a>, arguing that all government software must be explainable, is just one example of the talks I would have loved to see in person. There was also the already traditional QGIS Feature Frenzy by Kurt Menke and a <a href="https://fosstodon.org/@hansakwast/116854901329578202">QGIS hydrological analysis workshop</a> by Hans who also has a <a href="https://www.qwast-gis.com/l/foss4g-europe-2026/">full FOSS4G Europe 2026 summary</a> worth reading.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Also relevant: the Birds of a Feather session on AI in OSGeo projects has spilled over onto the <a href="https://lists.osgeo.org/pipermail/discuss/2026-July/040374.html">OSGeo discuss mailing list</a>. Definitely a thread worth following or getting involved in if you maintain or contribute to open source geospatial projects.</p>
<h2 class="wp-block-heading">What’s next</h2>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Besides MobiML, work also continues on the MovingPandas front. There are a few <a href="https://github.com/movingpandas/movingpandas/pulls">open pull requests</a> I want to work through ahead of the next release.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">After the summer break, the conference season picks back up quickly: <a href="https://foss4g-2026.github.io/en/">FOSS4G 2026</a> in Hiroshima (30 August–5 September), <a href="https://spatial-data-science.github.io/2026/">Spatial Data Science across Languages (SDSL) 2026</a> in Jena (16–17/18 September), and the <a href="https://conference.qgis.org">QGIS conference 2026</a> in Switzerland (5–6 October), where I’ll be speaking about AI in the QGIS ecosystem.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">For a more complete picture of what is going on in geospatial worldwide, check out (and don’t forget to bookmark) <a href="https://fosstodon.org/@nowosad">Jakub</a>‘s comprehensive list of geospatial conferences at <a href="https://github.com/Nowosad/geospatial-conferences">github.com/Nowosad/geospatial-conferences</a>.</p>
