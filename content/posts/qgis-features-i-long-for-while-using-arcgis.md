<h2>Transparency control</h2>
<blockquote><p>&#8220;ArcGIS have transparency! It&#8217;s in the Display tab, in the layer&#8217;s properties dialog!&#8221;</p></blockquote>
<p>Yes, but&#8230; you can only set transparency at the layer level. That is, either it&#8217;s all transparent, or it&#8217;s not&#8230;</p>
<p>In QGIS on the other end, you can set transparency at layer level, feature/symbol level, and color level. You can say that this is being overrated, but check the differences in the following images.</p>
<p><img alt="Transparency_layer_level" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-1709" height="510" src="https://gisunchained.files.wordpress.com/2016/01/transparency_layer_level.png" width="695" /><img alt="Transparency_feature_symbol_level" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-1716" height="510" src="https://gisunchained.files.wordpress.com/2016/01/transparency_feature_symbol_level.png" width="695" /><img alt="Transparency_color_level" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-1708" height="510" src="https://gisunchained.files.wordpress.com/2016/01/transparency_color_level.png" width="695" /></p>
<p>Notice that in QGIS you can set transparency at color level everywhere (or almost everywhere) there is a color to select. This includes annotations (like the ones in the image above), labels and composers items. You can even set transparency in colors by using the RGBA function in an expression! How sweet can that be? <img alt="ðŸ™‚" class="wp-smiley" src="https://s0.wp.com/wp-content/mu-plugins/wpcom-smileys/twemoji/2/72x72/1f642.png" style="height: 1em;" /></p>

<h2>Blending modes</h2>
<p>This is one of QGIS&#8217;s pristine jewels. The ability to combine layers the way you would do in almost any design/photoÂ editingÂ software. At layer or at feature level, you can control how they will &#8220;interact&#8221; with other layers or features below. Besides the normal mode, QGIS offers 12 other blending modes:Â  Lighten, Screen, Dodge, Darken, Multiply, Burn, Overlay, Soft light, Hard light, Difference, and Subtract. Check <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blend_modes">this page</a> to know more about the math behind each one and <a href="http://image.slidesharecdn.com/blendmodesforopengl-140213092122-phpapp02/95/blend-modes-for-opengl-3-638.jpg?cb=1392283377">this image</a> for some examples</p>
<p>It&#8217;s not easy to grasp how can this be of any use for cartography before you try it yourself. I had the chance to play around while trying to answer <a href="http://gis.stackexchange.com/a/82815/6191">this question</a>.</p>
<p><img alt="2wph4" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-1789" height="800" src="https://gisunchained.files.wordpress.com/2016/01/2wph4.jpg" width="800" /></p>
<p>A very common application for this functionality is when you want to add shadows to simulate the relief by putting a hill shade on top of other layers. In ArcGIS, you can only control the layer transparency, and the result is always a bit pale. But in QGIS, you can keep the strength of the original colors by using the multiply mode in the hill shade layer.</p>

<h3>Vector symbols renderers &#8220;powertools&#8221;</h3>
<p>In ArcGIS, you have a few fancy ways to symbol your vector layers. You got: <em>Single symbol</em>, <em>Unique values</em>, <em>Unique values many fields</em>&#8230; and so on. At the first glance, you may think that QGIS lacks some of them. Believe me, it doesn&#8217;t!Â <span style="line-height: 1.7;">In fact, QGIS offers much more flexibility when you need to symbol your layers.</span></p>
<p><span style="line-height: 1.7;"> For starters, it allows you to use fields or expressions on any of the symbols renderers, whileÂ ArcGIS only allows the use of fields.Â </span><span style="line-height: 1.7;">Powered by hundreds of functions and the ability to create your owns in python, what you can do with the expression builder has no limits.Â </span>This means, for instance, that you can combine, select, recalculate, normalize an infinite number of fields to create your own &#8220;values&#8221; (not to mention that you can tweak your values labels, making it ideal toÂ createÂ the legend).</p>
<p><figure class="wp-caption alignnone" id="attachment_1580" style="width: 825px;"><img alt="Screenshot from 2016-01-20 22:34:54" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-1580" height="570" src="https://gisunchained.files.wordpress.com/2016/01/screenshot-from-2016-01-20-223454.png" width="825" /><figcaption class="wp-caption-text" id="caption-attachment-1580">QGIS Graduated renderer using an expression to calculate population density</figcaption></figure></p>
<p>And then, in QGIS, you have the special (and kinda very specific) renderers, that make you say &#8220;wooooh&#8221;. Like the <em>Inverted polygons</em> that allow you to fill the the outside of polygons (nice to mask other features), the <em>Point displacement</em> to show points that are too close to each others, and the <em>Heatmap</em> that will transform, ON-THE-FLY, all your points in a layer into a nice heatmap without the need to convert them to raster (and that will update as soon as you, or anyone else, edits the point features).</p>
<p><figure class="wp-caption alignnone" id="attachment_1583" style="width: 777px;"><img alt="Screenshot from 2016-01-20 22:58:44" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-1583" height="543" src="https://gisunchained.files.wordpress.com/2016/01/screenshot-from-2016-01-20-225844.png" width="777" /><figcaption class="wp-caption-text" id="caption-attachment-1583">Inverted Polygon Renderer masking the outside of an interest area</figcaption></figure></p>
<p>But I have left the best toÂ the end. The &#8220;One rendered to Rule them all&#8221;, the <em>Rule-based</em> symbols. WithÂ the rule-based renderer, you can addÂ several rules, group themÂ in a tree structure, and setÂ each one with a desired symbol. This gives QGIS users fullÂ control of their layer&#8217;sÂ symbols, and, paired with expression builder and data-defined properties, opens the door to many wonderful applications.</p>

<h2>Atlas</h2>
<p>One of my favorite (and missed) features in QGIS is the Map Composer&#8217;s Atlas. I know that ArcGIS has its own &#8220;Atlas&#8221;, the Data Driven Pages, but frankly, it&#8217;s simply not the same.</p>
<p>I believe you know the basic functionally that both software allow. You create a map layout and choose a vector layer as coverage, and it will create an output panned or zoomed for each of the layer&#8217;s feature. You can also add some labels that will change according to the layers attributes.</p>
<p><strong>But in QGIS, things go a little bit further&#8230;</strong></p>
<p>Basically, you can use coverage layer&#8217;s attributes andÂ geometry anywhere you can use an expression. And, in QGIS, <span style="text-decoration: underline;">expressions are everywhere</span>. That way,Â mostÂ layers andÂ map composer items properties can be controlled by a single coverage layer.</p>
<p>With the right configuration, while iterating over the atlas coverage features, you can, Â <a href="http://nathanw.net/2013/12/02/waiting-for-qgis-2-2-highlighting-current-atlas-feature/">choose what feature to show and what features to hide</a>, <a href="http://nathanw.net/2014/09/23/qgis-atlas-on-non-geometry-tables/">change a theme color for your map</a>, <a href="https://gisunchained.wordpress.com/2014/11/09/series-de-mapas-com-formatos-multiplos-em-qgis-2-6-parte-1-multiple-format-map-series-using-qgis-2-6-part-1/">rotate and resize your page acording to the feature size</a>,Â <a href="http://nyalldawson.net/2013/04/a-neat-trick-in-qgis-2-0-images-in-atlas-prints/">choose a specific logo to came along with your map</a>, and much more. Once again, the sky is the limit.</p>
<p><figure class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_535" style="width: 506px;"><img alt="mosaico_regioes_fixed" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-535" height="433" src="https://gisunchained.files.wordpress.com/2014/11/mosaico_regioes_fixed.png" width="506" /><figcaption class="wp-caption-text" id="caption-attachment-535">Auto-resized maps that fits the coverage features at specific scale using atlas</figcaption></figure></p>
<p>So, if you pair <strong>Atlas</strong> it with QGIS <strong>data-defined properties</strong>,<strong> rule-based symbols</strong> and <strong>expressions</strong>, ArcGIS Data Driven Pages are no match. You don&#8217;t think so? Try to answer <a href="http://gis.stackexchange.com/questions/174817/feature-label-visibility-based-on-spatial-relationship-to-index-feature-using-da?atw=1">this question</a> then.</p>
<p><strong>Tip:</strong> If you really want to leverage your map production, using <a href="https://www.gaia-gis.it/gaia-sins/">Spatialite</a> or <a href="http://postgis.net/">Postgis </a>databases you can create the perfect atlas coverage layers from views that fitÂ your needs. No data redundancy and they are always updated.</p>

<p>In QGIS, like everything else, WFS is absolutely free (as in freedom, not free beer). All you need to do is add the WFS server&#8217;s URL, and you can add all the layers you want, with the absolute sure that they are asÂ updated as you can get.</p>
